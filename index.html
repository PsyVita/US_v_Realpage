Part II

The Plaintiff's Case

Sherman Act §§ 1 & 2
P1 · The Scheme

The Threat to American Renters

  • Housing as a Right, Not a Commodity: Millions of American renters depend on competitive pricing — but landlords replaced the free market with an algorithmic price-fixing cartel.
  • Data Pooling: Competing landlords secretly pool non-public, sensitive data — occupancy rates, lease expirations, concession offers — into RealPage's central algorithm.
  • "Autopilot" Pricing: The algorithm dictates daily inflated rent recommendations adopted by up to 90% of participating landlords, with minimal human override.
  • A Cartel by Algorithm: What was once independent competitive pricing became coordinated artificial inflation — a per se Sherman Act violation.
P2 · Sherman Act §§ 1 & 2

The "Data Moat" Monopoly

  • §1 — Price Fixing: Algorithms do not launder illegal conduct. Sharing competitor pricing data through shared software is per se horizontal price-fixing under the Sherman Act.
  • §2 — Monopolization: RealPage controls 80%+ of the algorithmic rental pricing market — illegally maintained through proprietary illicit data hoarding.
  • The "Data Moat": Years of pooled non-public data created an insurmountable competitive barrier — no rival can replicate this dataset, blocking all meaningful market entry.
  • The Harm & Remedy: Renters in 40+ states paid artificially inflated rents. The United States seeks to dismantle this cartel, break the data monopoly, and restore independent, fair market competition.
P3 · The Conduct & The Harm

From Independent Pricing to Cartel Control

1 · RealPage replaced independent pricing with coordinated pricing
  • In a competitive market, landlords make pricing decisions independently based on their own business judgment.
  • RealPage fundamentally changed this process by centralizing pricing decisions.
  • Landlords shared confidential pricing and occupancy data through one platform.
  • The algorithm used competitors' non-public information to generate pricing recommendations.
  • Although landlords could reject recommendations, RealPage encouraged high compliance and trust in the algorithm.
  • When many competing landlords rely on the same recommendation system, pricing becomes coordinated rather than truly independent.
  • This reduces the competitive pressure that normally keeps rents affordable.
2 · Harm to consumers
  • Antitrust law exists to protect competition because competition benefits consumers.
  • RealPage's conduct caused real harm to renters.
  • Tenants paid higher rents than they would have in a competitive market.
  • Discounts, concessions, and promotional offers became less common.
  • Some apartments were intentionally left vacant instead of being rented at lower prices.
  • As a result, families had fewer affordable housing options during a housing shortage.
  • The defense may argue that landlords were simply maximizing profits.
  • However, earning profits through fair competition is legal.
  • Coordinating prices using competitors' confidential information is not.
P4 · Impact & Conclusion

Beyond Housing — The Rule of Law

3 · Why this case matters beyond housing
  • This case is about more than apartment rents.
  • It raises an important question for the future of the digital economy.
  • Can companies use algorithms to achieve coordination that competitors could not legally achieve through direct communication?
  • If this conduct is allowed, similar practices could spread to other industries such as:
    • Hotels
    • Healthcare
    • Insurance
    • Transportation
    • Retail
  • Technology should encourage innovation, not undermine competition.
  • Algorithms must not become a loophole for price-fixing.
4 · Legal conclusion
  • The evidence establishes three key facts:
    • Competitors exchanged commercially sensitive information through RealPage.
    • That information was used to generate coordinated pricing recommendations across competing landlords.
    • Consumers paid higher rents and experienced less price competition.
  • These are exactly the kinds of harms the Sherman Act was designed to prevent.
  • The law protects consumers by preserving independent competition, regardless of whether coordination occurs through people or algorithms.
